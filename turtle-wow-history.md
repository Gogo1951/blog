# Shenna's History

*How one operator turned a fan hobby into a decade-long business -- quietly, and across three different names.*

## The setup

World of Warcraft is a twenty-year-old online game owned by Blizzard Entertainment. For most of that time, there's been a parallel ecosystem of *private servers* -- unauthorized fan-run copies of the game, usually hosting older versions that Blizzard has since retired. They're built on pirated code, run on donations, and operate in countries where Blizzard's lawyers can't reach them.

Most private servers are passion projects. A small number become actual businesses. The person who figured out how to make that transition work -- repeatedly -- went by the name *Shenna*, then later, *Torta*.

## The first run: Elysium

In 2017, Shenna was a senior administrator at *the Elysium Project*, the largest vanilla WoW private server of its time. Elysium inherited the player base of an earlier server, Nostalrius, which Blizzard had shut down the previous year. Tens of thousands of players were logging in daily. Donations flowed. The community trusted the staff.

That trust turned out to be worth about €2,000 in Shenna's pocket, quietly siphoned from the donation account over several months. Her co-administrator, Crogge, had a parallel operation going: duplicating in-game gold and high-level characters and selling them to third-party brokers for real money.

When the rest of the staff uncovered it, they staged a coup -- wiped the servers, took their backups, and walked out to start a clean rival project. Shenna and Crogge both admitted to everything in public statements, expressed hurt at being betrayed by their former colleagues, and announced they'd keep Elysium going anyway.

## The second run: Turtle WoW

A year later, a new vanilla server launched under the name *Turtle WoW*, run by someone called Torta. Longtime observers recognized the fingerprints immediately -- the infrastructure choices, the community management style, the eventual reappearance of Crogge in adjacent projects -- but Turtle was also, genuinely, good. Better than Elysium had been. Better, in many ways, than what Blizzard was shipping on its own official servers.

Turtle added original zones, custom classes, and new storylines. It peaked at 44,000 concurrent players and claimed roughly half a million registered accounts. It ran a "donation shop" that converted real money into in-game currency -- the same model every major private server now uses, and the same model Shenna was run out of Elysium for.

This time, she owned the thing. So it wasn't embezzlement. It was revenue.

## The pattern

Along the way, smaller servers kept quietly changing hands. Vanilla+ -- a project that had explicitly marketed itself as anti-pay-to-win -- was taken over by a new administrator who appeared from nowhere, added a cash shop, and disappeared. Everlook went through a similar shift. Community investigators pointed to the same operator behind all of them.

None of it is illegal in any jurisdiction that matters. Turtle operated out of Russia. The servers it absorbed were already unauthorized. Blizzard finally sued in 2025, won the case, and reached a settlement in April 2026 that shut Turtle down -- but the only thing that lawsuit actually proved is what everyone already knew: that a fan running pirated game code can't win in a U.S. court. It doesn't say much about what happens next, under the next name.

## The read

The uncomfortable part of the story isn't really the embezzlement. That was eight years ago, under a different handle, and it was small money. The uncomfortable part is watching someone figure out that a passionate volunteer community is a business opportunity, and then spending a decade executing on that insight -- absorbing other people's projects, flipping their business models, and moving the money offshore.

It's capitalism. It's legal-ish. It's also the kind of thing that quietly drains the oxygen out of the room every time it happens. The people building these projects because they love the game end up working, unpaid, for someone who figured out the exit first.
